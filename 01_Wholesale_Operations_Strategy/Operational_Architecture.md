# Operational Architecture

## Overview

Operational architecture defines how wholesale operations are structured to support order execution, system integration, logistics coordination, financial processing, and performance visibility.

In wholesale environments, operations serve as the internal execution engine that connects sales activity with inventory availability, fulfillment execution, shipment coordination, and invoicing accuracy. A strong operational architecture ensures that workflows are standardized, systems are aligned, and cross-functional teams can execute efficiently at scale.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of operational architecture is to create a structured model that supports consistent execution across the wholesale order lifecycle.

This includes:

- defining how orders move through internal systems
- identifying key operational control points
- aligning teams across order management, logistics, finance, and analytics
- ensuring workflows are documented and repeatable
- supporting continuous improvement through performance measurement

A well-designed operational architecture reduces process variability, improves accuracy, and supports scalability.

---

## Core Operational Layers

Wholesale operations can be organized into six primary layers:

### 1. Order Management

This layer manages the intake, validation, and processing of purchase orders.

Key responsibilities include:

- purchase order intake
- order validation
- pricing and SKU verification
- inventory checks
- order release to fulfillment

### 2. Systems Integration

This layer connects external partner systems with internal enterprise platforms.

Examples include:

- EDI platforms
- retailer portals
- ERP integrations
- inventory synchronization

### 3. Logistics and Fulfillment

This layer governs inventory allocation, warehouse processing, shipment coordination, and routing compliance.

Key responsibilities include:

- fulfillment planning
- shipment execution
- carrier coordination
- routing guide compliance

### 4. Financial Operations

This layer ensures orders that are shipped are invoiced accurately and tracked through payment completion.

Responsibilities include:

- invoice generation
- billing validation
- chargeback prevention
- payment tracking

### 5. Data and Analytics

This layer provides operational visibility through reporting, dashboards, and KPI monitoring.

Examples include:

- order status reporting
- inventory tracking
- shipment performance dashboards
- invoice accuracy reporting

### 6. Process Improvement and Governance

This layer supports long-term operational maturity through SOP development, KPI review, risk controls, and workflow optimization.

---

## End-to-End Operating Flow

A typical wholesale operating flow includes the following stages:

1. Purchase order receipt  
2. Order validation and quality assurance  
3. ERP order creation or EDI processing  
4. Inventory allocation  
5. Warehouse fulfillment  
6. Shipment execution and routing compliance  
7. Shipment confirmation  
8. Invoice generation  
9. Payment and reconciliation  

Each stage depends on system accuracy, cross-functional coordination, and clearly defined operational controls.

---

## Cross-Functional Alignment

Operational architecture depends on coordination across multiple teams.

Common stakeholders include:

- sales and account management
- order operations
- warehouse and fulfillment
- logistics and transportation
- finance and billing
- systems and IT support
- inventory planning

The architecture works best when responsibilities are clearly defined and communication paths are documented.

---

## Control Points

Operational control points are checkpoints that reduce errors and protect execution quality.

Important control points include:

- purchase order validation before entry
- inventory availability checks before allocation
- routing compliance review before shipment
- invoice validation before transmission
- KPI review after execution

These controls help reduce downstream issues such as shipment delays, chargebacks, inventory conflicts, and billing disputes.

---

## Performance Measurement

Operational architecture should be supported by measurable performance indicators.

Examples include:

- order accuracy rate
- fill rate
- on-time shipment rate
- routing compliance rate
- invoice accuracy rate
- chargeback rate

Performance data allows organizations to identify bottlenecks and improve execution over time.

---

## Final Note

Operational architecture provides the foundation for scalable wholesale operations. When workflows, systems, responsibilities, and control points are aligned, organizations can support order accuracy, reliable fulfillment, financial integrity, and operational visibility across the full wholesale lifecycle.
