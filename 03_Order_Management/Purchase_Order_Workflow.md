# Purchase Order Workflow

## Overview

The purchase order (PO) workflow defines the internal process used to receive, validate, process, and execute wholesale orders from external partners such as retailers, distributors, or marketplaces.

A structured workflow ensures that orders move through internal systems accurately and efficiently while minimizing operational errors, shipment delays, and financial discrepancies.

Wholesale operations teams are responsible for managing this workflow and coordinating across sales, fulfillment, logistics, and finance teams.

---

# Objectives of the PO Workflow

The purchase order workflow is designed to achieve the following operational objectives:

• Ensure all incoming orders are validated before system entry  
• Prevent SKU, pricing, or quantity errors  
• Confirm inventory availability before fulfillment  
• Maintain routing and compliance requirements  
• Enable smooth coordination between internal departments  
• Ensure accurate invoicing and financial reconciliation  

---

# Purchase Order Intake

Purchase orders can enter the organization through multiple channels depending on partner integration capabilities.

Common intake methods include:

• EDI order transmission  
• Retail partner portals  
• Wholesale ordering platforms  
• Email-based order submission  
• Sales representative order entry  

Regardless of the intake method, all purchase orders must be validated before being processed.

---

# Purchase Order Validation Checklist

Before an order is processed, operations teams should perform a structured validation check.

### Product Validation

Confirm that:

• SKUs exist in the ERP system  
• SKUs match the retailer catalog  
• Product descriptions align with order details

### Pricing Validation

Verify that:

• Unit pricing matches contracted pricing  
• Promotional pricing is applied correctly  
• Discounts or terms are consistent with agreements

### Quantity Validation

Ensure:

• Order quantities are realistic and expected  
• Case pack requirements are met  
• Minimum order quantities are satisfied if applicable

### Inventory Availability

Confirm:

• Available-to-sell (ATS) inventory levels  
• Allocation requirements for high-demand SKUs  
• Backorder rules if inventory is unavailable

### Routing Compliance

Verify:

• Retailer routing guide requirements  
• shipping methods  
• labeling requirements  
• palletization or carton requirements

---

# Order Processing

Once the PO passes validation checks, the order is entered into the enterprise system.

Typical order processing steps include:

1. Create order record in ERP system  
2. Attach customer or retailer identifier  
3. Assign shipping and billing details  
4. Allocate inventory  
5. Confirm fulfillment location  
6. Release order for warehouse processing

---

# Inventory Allocation

Inventory allocation ensures product availability before fulfillment begins.

Operations teams must confirm:

• available warehouse inventory  
• reserved quantities for other orders  
• inventory in transit if applicable

Allocation rules may prioritize:

• key retail partners  
• high revenue orders  
• seasonal product launches

---

# Fulfillment Release

Once inventory allocation is confirmed, orders are released to the warehouse or third-party logistics provider (3PL).

Warehouse systems will then perform:

• picking  
• packing  
• labeling  
• shipment preparation

---

# Shipment Confirmation

After orders are fulfilled, shipment confirmation is generated.

This step may include:

• advanced shipment notification (ASN)  
• tracking information  
• carton or pallet details  
• shipment documentation

Shipment confirmation data may be transmitted through EDI or retailer platforms.

---

# Invoice Creation

Once the shipment is confirmed, the finance system generates an invoice.

The invoice should reflect:

• shipped quantities  
• pricing terms  
• payment terms  
• shipping charges if applicable

Invoices may also be transmitted through EDI depending on the partner integration.

---

# Operational Control Points

Key control points within the purchase order workflow include:

• PO validation before order entry  
• inventory verification before allocation  
• routing compliance checks before shipment  
• invoice verification before transmission

These control points help prevent operational issues such as:

• shipment delays  
• retailer chargebacks  
• order rejections  
• billing discrepancies

---

# Continuous Improvement

Wholesale operations teams should regularly review the PO workflow to identify opportunities for improvement.

Areas of focus may include:

• automation of order intake  
• reduction of manual entry errors  
• improved system integrations  
• streamlined communication between departments

Operational metrics and analytics should be used to identify bottlenecks and optimize workflow efficiency.
